    33→1.1 Introduction
    34→
    35→Blood is a vital and irreplaceable biological resource. Unlike most
    36→medical supplies, it cannot be manufactured synthetically and must be
    37→voluntarily donated, carefully stored, and efficiently distributed to
    38→patients who need it (World Health Organization [WHO], 2022). Blood
    39→transfusions are essential across a wide range of clinical situations,
    40→including emergency trauma care, surgical procedures, obstetric
    41→haemorrhage, and the management of chronic disorders such as sickle cell
    42→disease (Kassebaum et al., 2016). The institution responsible for
    43→collecting, testing, storing, and distributing blood is known as a blood
    44→bank, and its effective management is directly linked to patient
    45→survival outcomes (Daniels and Bromilow, 2014).
    46→
    47→In many developing countries, including Ghana, blood bank operations
    48→continue to rely on manual, paper-based processes or disconnected
    49→spreadsheet systems. These approaches are error-prone, offer no
    50→real-time visibility of stock, and make cross-facility coordination
    51→nearly impossible (Olarewaju et al., 2020). The consequences are well
    52→documented: blood wastage from poor expiry monitoring, delayed
    53→transfusions during emergencies, and patients being referred between
    54→hospitals without any confirmed availability of the required blood type
    55→at the receiving facility.
    56→
    57→This project proposes the design and implementation of a Cloud-Based
    58→Blood Bank Management System, enabling registered hospitals and clinics
    59→to manage their own blood inventory while sharing real-time blood
    60→availability across the network. The system introduces a broadcast
    61→request model where a facility in need of blood sends a request to all
    62→registered hospitals on the network, those with available stock respond,
    63→and the requesting facility selects the most suitable match and reserves
    64→the blood unit for the incoming patient. This directly addresses the
    65→blind referral problem identified through primary research conducted as
    66→part of this project.
    67→
    68→1.2 Background of the Study
    69→
    70→Blood bank management has historically depended on paper registers,
    71→manual donor records, and physical stock ledgers. While these methods
    72→function in low-volume environments, they are characterised by
    73→significant weaknesses including data entry errors, inability to track
    74→inventory in real time, difficulties in donor follow-up, and delayed
    75→blood request processing (Agrawal et al., 2021). As healthcare demands
    76→have grown, the inadequacy of these manual systems has become a pressing
    77→operational and clinical concern (WHO, 2021).
    78→
    79→In Ghana, the National Blood Service Ghana (NBSG), established under Act
    80→850 of 2013, is the statutory body responsible for ensuring a safe and
    81→adequate national blood supply. Despite institutional efforts, the NBSG
    82→collected approximately 187,000 units of blood in 2024 against an
    83→estimated national requirement of 308,000 units, representing a
    84→shortfall of approximately 40% (National Blood Service Ghana, 2023).
    85→Fragmented, paper-based record-keeping in hospitals and blood collection
    86→centres is a contributing factor, as it prevents efficient stock
    87→monitoring, donor scheduling, and inter-facility coordination (Abdulai
    88→et al., 2020).
    89→
    90→A further infrastructural gap was identified during this project's
    91→preliminary research. Government hospitals in Ghana use the Lightwave
    92→Health Information Management System (LMHIS) for managing patient
    93→records and general administrative functions. However, LMHIS does not
    94→include a blood bank module, meaning that even digitally equipped
    95→facilities lack a dedicated system for blood inventory tracking, expiry
    96→monitoring, or cross-facility blood availability sharing. This gap
    97→directly motivated the scope of the proposed system.
    98→
    99→1.3 Problem Statement
   100→
   101→Despite the critical role of blood transfusion services in modern
   102→healthcare, many blood banks in developing countries, including Ghana,
   103→continue to rely on manual and fragmented systems for managing blood
   104→storage, distribution, and hospital requests. These traditional
   105→approaches are inefficient and unable to support the increasing demand
   106→for fast, accurate, and coordinated blood management services (Olarewaju
   107→et al., 2020). The dependence on paper-based records and isolated
   108→facility-level processes creates significant challenges, including
   109→inaccurate inventory records, delayed communication between healthcare
   110→facilities, poor tracking of blood units, and difficulties in making
   111→timely decisions during emergencies.
   112→
   113→The limitations of existing blood bank management practices affect
   114→several critical areas of transfusion services. Manual recording of
   115→blood collection, testing, storage, and distribution increases the
   116→likelihood of human errors, including incorrect blood unit information
   117→and inaccurate patient matching, which may compromise transfusion safety
   118→(Stainsby et al., 2006; Bolton-Maggs et al., 2012). Furthermore, the
   119→absence of real-time inventory monitoring makes it difficult for blood
   120→banks and hospitals to know the availability, location, and expiry
   121→status of blood units, contributing to unnecessary wastage and shortages
   122→during urgent medical situations (Agrawal et al., 2021; Dumont et al.,
   123→2015).
   124→
   125→Another major challenge is the lack of an interconnected platform that
   126→allows hospitals and blood banks within a region to communicate
   127→effectively. Currently, healthcare facilities may experience delays when
   128→searching for available blood units from other institutions, resulting
   129→in slower response times during emergencies. The absence of a
   130→centralised digital system also limits the ability to generate accurate
   131→reports, analyse blood demand patterns, and support effective planning
   132→and decision-making (Emmanuel et al., 2021; WHO, 2021).
   133→
   134→A primary survey conducted in June 2026 among 14 healthcare
   135→professionals across clinics, district hospitals, regional hospitals,
   136→and teaching hospitals confirmed that 21.4% had experienced a patient
   137→being referred for blood only for it to be unavailable upon arrival,
   138→while a further 35.7% were unsure, meaning up to 57.1% of respondents
   139→could not rule out a failed referral at their facility (Healthcare
   140→Facility Blood Management Survey, 2026). A field interview at Alajo
   141→Community Clinic further confirmed that referrals are frequently made
   142→using personal contacts who may not answer during emergencies, resulting
   143→in blind patient transfers.
   144→
   145→To address these challenges, this project proposes the development of a
   146→cloud-based, multi-facility Blood Bank Management System that enables
   147→healthcare facilities to manage blood inventory, monitor blood
   148→availability in real time, and communicate efficiently with other
   149→hospitals within a connected network through a structured broadcast
   150→request model.

   159→blood requests across a network of registered facilities, and support
   160→informed patient referrals through confirmed cross-facility blood
   161→availability, thereby reducing blood wastage, failed referrals, and
   162→preventable patient deaths.

   198→
   199→The significance of this study lies in its potential to address a
   200→documented and life-threatening gap in Ghana's blood management
   201→infrastructure. By enabling real-time cross-facility blood visibility
   202→and a structured broadcast request model, the system directly eliminates
   203→the blind referral problem confirmed by this project's primary research,
   204→in which up to 57.1% of surveyed facilities could not rule out a failed
   205→referral having occurred (Healthcare Facility Blood Management Survey,
   206→2026).
   207→
   208→From an operational standpoint, the system automates blood inventory
   209→tracking, expiry monitoring, storage management, and request processing,
   210→replacing manual and spreadsheet-based methods that offer no automated
   211→safeguards. Academically, this project demonstrates the application of
   212→modern cloud technologies to a real, validated healthcare problem,
   213→contributing to the growing field of health informatics in the West
   214→African context. It also provides a working model that could inform the
   215→future development of a dedicated blood bank module within platforms
   216→such as LMHIS.

   218→1.6 Justification of the Study
   219→
   220→The need for this system is justified on three grounds. First, from a
   221→patient safety perspective, the risks associated with manual blood

   225→infrastructure (LMHIS) has no blood bank module, and no existing

   227→broadcast request system. Third, the 92.9% demand confirmation from
   228→healthcare workers in the project's primary survey provides
   229→near-unanimous stakeholder validation for the system's development

   256→CHAPTER TWO: LITERATURE REVIEW
   257→
   258→Introduction
   259→
   260→Healthcare facilities across the world are increasingly transitioning
   261→from traditional paper-based blood bank registers to digital blood
   262→management information systems. As healthcare institutions expand and
   263→the demand for safe, timely blood transfusion grows, the limitations of
   264→paper-based systems have made digital solutions such as Blood Bank
   265→Management Systems essential for efficient transfusion service delivery
   266→(WHO, 2021; Agrawal et al., 2021). This chapter reviews relevant
   267→literature related to blood bank management systems, discusses the
   268→Technology Acceptance Model as a guiding theoretical framework, reviews
   269→previous studies and related works, and identifies gaps that justify the
   270→proposed system.
   271→
   272→2.1 Overview of Relevant Literature
   273→
   274→2.1.1 Concept of Blood Bank Management Systems
   275→
   276→A Blood Bank Management System is a digital platform used by hospitals
   277→and blood collection centres to record, store, and manage information
   278→related to blood donation, screening, storage, inventory, and issuance.
   279→According to the World Health Organization, an effective blood
   280→management information system should capture the full chain of custody
   281→of a blood unit, from donor screening through to final issuance to a
   282→patient (WHO, 2021). Modern systems provide structured fields and
   283→validation checks ensuring that records are complete, standardised, and
   284→verifiable before a unit is issued for transfusion (Bolton-Maggs et al.,
   285→2012). The development of blood bank information systems can be traced
   286→to early hospital laboratory information systems introduced from the
   287→1980s onward, which later expanded to include donor management,
   288→cross-matching support, and expiry monitoring (Stainsby et al., 2006).
   289→
   290→2.1.2 Benefits of Blood Bank Management Systems
   291→
   292→Blood Bank Management Systems provide several important benefits.
   293→Digital systems provide live inventory dashboards allowing blood bank
   294→officers to view stock levels by blood group and Rhesus factor at any
   295→moment, directly improving inventory accuracy (Agrawal et al., 2021).
   296→Automated expiry alerts flag units approaching their shelf life,
   297→allowing staff to prioritise their use and directly reducing wastage
   298→(Dumont et al., 2015). These systems also enhance communication between
   299→hospital departments and facilities, enable faster decision-making
   300→during emergencies, and support long-term planning through historical
   301→demand data (Emmanuel et al., 2021; WHO, 2021; Stainsby et al., 2006).
   302→
   303→2.1.3 Blood Bank Systems in Africa
   304→
   305→The adoption of digital blood bank management systems across Africa has
   306→grown gradually over the past two decades. Progesa, developed by
   307→MAK-SYSTEM, is among the most widely deployed blood establishment
   308→platforms used by national blood transfusion services, though
   309→deployments typically depend on sustained donor funding and remain
   310→outside the reach of hospital-level facilities (WHO, 2021). South
   311→Africa's National Blood Service operates one of the more advanced
   312→systems on the continent, though it requires significant sustained
   313→investment not readily available to most other countries (Olarewaju et
   314→al., 2020). In East Africa, national blood transfusion services in
   315→countries such as Kenya have introduced computerised systems primarily
   316→for HIV screening data management, but these have generally remained
   317→centred on regional blood banks rather than extending real-time stock
   318→visibility to district hospitals and clinics (WHO, 2021). Consistent
   319→challenges across all deployments include unreliable electricity, poor
   320→connectivity, high staff turnover, and shortages of trained blood bank
   321→information personnel.
   322→
   323→2.1.4 Blood Bank Systems in Ghana
   324→
   325→Ghana has made gradual progress in digitising its health information
   326→landscape, but blood bank management specifically remains one of the
   327→least digitised areas of the country's healthcare system. DHIMS2,
   328→Ghana's most widely deployed digital health platform, only supports
   329→aggregate reporting and cannot manage individual blood units, donor
   330→records, or facility-level inventory, making it unsuitable for blood
   331→bank operations (Karuri et al., 2014). LHIMS, deployed in Ghana's major
   332→teaching hospitals, supports patient registration and clinical
   333→documentation but does not include a blood bank module. This gap was
   334→directly confirmed through this project's preliminary research and forms
   335→a central part of the justification for the proposed system. The
   336→project's own primary survey found that 71.4% of facilities record blood
   337→stock using a computer or spreadsheet and 21.4% still use paper, with no
   338→facility reporting use of a dedicated blood bank management system
   339→(Healthcare Facility Blood Management Survey, 2026).
   340→
   341→2.2 Theoretical Framework
   342→
   343→For this study, the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) is adopted as the
   344→guiding theoretical framework to explain the factors that will influence
   345→healthcare professionals' acceptance and use of the proposed system. TAM
   346→is relevant because the system introduces a significant change in how
   347→blood bank officers and laboratory technicians record, retrieve, and
   348→share blood inventory information, and the success of this change
   349→depends on how staff perceive the system.
   350→
   351→2.2.1 Overview of the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)
   352→
   353→The Technology Acceptance Model was developed by Fred D. Davis in 1989,
   354→drawing from the Theory of Reasoned Action. TAM proposes that a user's
   355→actual use of a technology is determined by their behavioural intention,
   356→shaped by two core beliefs: Perceived Usefulness (PU) and Perceived Ease
   357→of Use (PEOU) (Davis, 1989). Venkatesh and Davis (2000) later extended
   358→the model, demonstrating its validity across various organisational and
   359→technological contexts and establishing it as one of the most
   360→empirically validated models in information systems research.
   361→
   362→2.2.2 Perceived Usefulness (PU)
   363→
   364→Perceived usefulness refers to the degree to which a person believes
   365→that using a particular system will enhance their job performance
   366→(Davis, 1989). The proposed system's cloud-based cross-facility
   367→architecture directly strengthens perceived usefulness by enabling
   368→instant stock verification, eliminating the personal contact-based
   369→referral process confirmed as unreliable in this project's survey where
   370→57.1% of respondents relied on contacts to confirm blood availability
   371→(Healthcare Facility Blood Management Survey, 2026). Automated expiry
   372→alerts, the broadcast request model, and cloud-based backups further
   373→enhance the system's perceived operational value to blood bank staff.
   374→Studies have consistently shown that healthcare professionals are more
   375→likely to adopt digital systems they perceive as genuinely improving
   376→efficiency and patient care safety (Holden and Karsh, 2010).
   377→
   378→2.2.3 Perceived Ease of Use (PEOU)
   379→
   380→Perceived ease of use refers to the degree to which a user believes that
   381→interacting with a system will require minimal effort (Davis, 1989). In
   382→blood bank settings, ease of use is especially critical since staff work
   383→under significant time pressure during emergencies. Any system that is
   384→complex or slow to navigate risks being abandoned in favour of familiar
   385→manual alternatives, a pattern documented in healthcare environments
   386→where usability challenges affect adoption (Olarewaju et al., 2020).
   387→Research by Holden and Karsh (2010) confirmed that ease of use plays a
   388→key role in healthcare technology adoption, a principle that directly
   389→informs the interface design of the proposed system.
   390→
   391→2.2.4 Application of TAM in Health Information Systems
   392→
   393→TAM has been widely applied in health information systems research
   394→examining the adoption of electronic medical records, laboratory
   395→information systems, and hospital management systems. Holden and Karsh
   396→(2010) reported that user acceptance is a critical factor in the
   397→successful implementation of healthcare technologies and that even
   398→technically sound systems fail if staff do not perceive them as useful
   399→or easy to use. In this study, TAM provides the framework for ensuring
   400→that the proposed system is designed around the operational realities of
   401→blood bank staff rather than purely technical specifications.
   402→
   403→2.3 Previous Studies and Related Works
   404→
   405→2.3.1 SafeTrace Tx Blood Establishment Computer Software
   406→
   407→SafeTrace Tx, supported under Haemonetics, is one of the most widely
   408→adopted Blood Establishment Computer Software platforms in
   409→well-resourced healthcare systems, providing comprehensive functionality
   410→covering donor management, blood typing and crossmatch support,
   411→inventory control, and regulatory compliance (WHO, 2021). Despite its
   412→advanced capabilities, these platforms are expensive to implement and
   413→maintain, require significant local server infrastructure, and are
   414→designed for single-facility deployment with no built-in mechanism for
   415→cross-facility blood availability sharing, a core requirement identified
   416→through this project's primary research.
   417→
   418→2.3.2 e-RaktKosh National Blood Bank Management System
   419→
   420→e-RaktKosh is a centralised, cloud-based blood bank management system
   421→developed by India's Ministry of Health under the National Health
   422→Mission. The platform connects blood banks across the country into a
   423→single network, representing one of the closest real-world parallels to
   424→the system proposed in this project (WHO, 2021). Despite its strengths,
   425→e-RaktKosh depends on substantial government investment and sustained
   426→institutional coordination at a national scale not currently available
   427→in Ghana, and does not include a structured broadcast-and-response
   428→workflow where a requesting facility actively receives direct responses
   429→from facilities with matching stock.
   430→
   431→2.3.3 DHIMS2 District Health Information Management System
   432→
   433→DHIMS2 is Ghana's primary national digital health information platform,
   434→used to collect and analyse health data at district, regional, and
   435→national levels. Despite its national reach, DHIMS2 only supports
   436→aggregate health services data and was not designed for unit-level blood
   437→inventory management, donor tracking, or facility-to-facility blood
   438→requests (Karuri et al., 2014), making it entirely unsuitable as a blood
   439→bank management solution.
   440→
   441→2.3.4 LHIMS Local Health Information Management System
   442→
   443→LHIMS is a patient-level hospital management system deployed in Ghana's
   444→major teaching hospitals, supporting patient registration, clinical
   445→documentation, appointment management, and billing (Adokiya et al.,
   446→2015). Despite its considerable strengths, LHIMS does not include a
   447→blood bank module of any kind. Blood inventory, donor records, expiry
   448→monitoring, and blood request processing are entirely outside the scope
   449→of LHIMS as currently deployed. Its deployment has also remained limited
   450→to a small number of large teaching hospitals, leaving most facilities
   451→without access to any comparable digital system.

   453→2.4 How the Proposed System Addresses Identified Gaps
   454→
   455→2.4.1 Addressing Blind Referral Through Cross-Facility Broadcast Integration
   456→
   457→No existing system available and deployed in Ghana brings cross-facility
   458→blood visibility and hospital-level inventory management together. The
   459→proposed system directly addresses this by integrating blood unit
   460→inventory, expiry monitoring, and a structured broadcast request module
   461→into a single cloud-hosted platform shared across registered facilities,
   462→directly resolving the blind referral failure confirmed in this
   463→project's primary research where up to 57.1% of surveyed facilities
   464→could not rule out a failed referral having occurred (Healthcare
   465→Facility Blood Management Survey, 2026).
   466→
   467→2.4.2 Addressing Accessibility Through Cloud-Based Architecture
   468→
   469→The proposed system eliminates the infrastructure and cost barrier of
   470→deployment entirely through its cloud-based architecture. A hospital
   471→requires only internet access and a standard web browser to access the
   472→full system, making it deployable across a far wider range of healthcare
   473→facilities than existing solutions, from teaching hospitals to smaller
   474→district facilities and clinics.
   475→
   476→2.4.3 Addressing Usability Through TAM-Informed Design
   477→
   478→The proposed system was designed with the Technology Acceptance Model as
   479→its guiding framework, using role-based dashboards so each user sees
   480→only the modules relevant to their role, and ensuring that critical
   481→tasks such as checking stock by blood group or responding to a broadcast
   482→request are accessible within a few clicks from the main dashboard,
   483→directly targeting the usability failures that have undermined adoption
   484→of comparable systems elsewhere.
   485→
   486→2.4.4 Addressing Downtime Through Cloud Reliability and Local Caching
   487→
   488→Cloud hosting provides significantly higher reliability and uptime than
   489→locally hosted servers, with automated backups ensuring inventory data
   490→is never permanently lost. A local caching mechanism allows essential
   491→blood stock data to be temporarily stored on the local device during
   492→connectivity disruptions and automatically synchronised with the central
   493→database once the connection is restored.
   494→
   495→2.4.5 Addressing Security Through Role-Based Access and Compliance with Ghanaian Law
   496→
   497→The proposed system implements strict role-based access control,
   498→comprehensive audit trails, HTTPS encryption, and explicit compliance
   499→with Ghana's Data Protection Act, 2012 (Act 843), providing a legal and
   500→ethical foundation for blood and donor data management that many
   501→existing facility-level systems in Ghana have not addressed.
   502→
   503→2.4.6 What Makes the Proposed System Different
   504→
   505→What distinguishes the proposed system from all existing solutions
   506→reviewed is the combination of cross-facility broadcast integration,
   507→cloud accessibility, TAM-informed usability, and local context awareness
   508→in a single platform. Existing systems address one or two of these
   509→dimensions but not all of them together. The proposed system was built
   510→specifically to bring all these qualities together in a solution
   511→tailored to the realities of Ghanaian healthcare, making it not just
   512→technically functional but genuinely deployable and adoptable across
   513→hospitals and clinics of varying size and resource level.
   514→
   515→2.5 Conclusion
   516→
   517→This chapter provided a comprehensive review of blood bank and related
   518→health information management systems. The review revealed a consistent
   519→pattern: while considerable progress has been made in digitising health
   520→information generally, blood bank management specifically remains
   521→underserved in Ghana. The Technology Acceptance Model highlighted that
   522→system success depends on how users perceive its usefulness and ease of
   523→use, reinforcing the importance of TAM-informed design. These findings
   524→justify the development of the proposed cloud-based, multi-facility
   525→Blood Bank Management System. The next chapter presents the methodology,
   526→system specification, and design of the proposed solution.

   528→CHAPTER THREE: SYSTEM SPECIFICATION AND DESIGN
   529→
   530→Introduction
   531→
   532→This chapter presents the system specification and design of the
   533→proposed Cloud-Based Blood Bank Management System. It begins by
   534→describing the software development methodology adopted for the project.
   535→It then proceeds to the system specification, covering the analysis of
   536→requirements, stakeholder analysis, functional requirements,
   537→non-functional requirements, and use cases. The chapter concludes with
   538→the system design, detailing the architectural design, database design,
   539→user interface design, and security considerations. Together, these
   540→elements form the technical blueprint upon which the system
   541→implementation described in Chapter Four is based.
   542→
   543→3.1 Methodology
   544→
   545→The development of the Cloud-Based Blood Bank Management System followed
   546→the Agile Software Development methodology, specifically an iterative
   547→approach organised into four development sprints. Agile was selected
   548→because it allows for incremental development, continuous feedback, and
   549→the flexibility to refine requirements as understanding of the problem
   550→deepens, making it particularly well suited to a project grounded in
   551→ongoing primary research (Sommerville, 2016). Rather than attempting to
   552→fully define and freeze all requirements before writing any code, the
   553→Agile approach allowed the development team to begin building core
   554→modules early and revisit design decisions as insights emerged from the
   555→survey research, field interviews, and supervisor feedback.
   556→
   557→Sprint 1: Requirements and Design (Weeks 1 to 2)
   558→
   559→The first sprint focused on finalising system requirements through the
   560→primary survey, field interview at Alajo Community Clinic, review of
   561→existing systems documented in Chapter Two, and stakeholder
   562→consultations. System architecture, database schema, and interface
   563→wireframes were produced during this sprint, forming the design

   598→3.2 System Specification
   599→
   600→3.2.1 Analysis of Requirements
   601→
   602→Requirements analysis was conducted through three complementary methods.
   603→First, the primary survey administered to 14 healthcare professionals
   604→provided quantitative data confirming the key operational gaps in
   605→existing blood management practices, including the absence of real-time
   606→inventory visibility, the failure of personal contact-based referral
   607→systems, and the complete absence of cross-facility blood availability
   608→sharing. Second, the field interview at Alajo Community Clinic provided
   609→qualitative depth, describing in concrete terms how the current referral
   610→process breaks down during emergencies. Third, a review of existing
   611→systems, covering SafeTrace Tx, e-RaktKosh, DHIMS2, and LHIMS, revealed
   612→specific functional gaps that the proposed system is designed to
   613→address.
   614→
   615→From these three sources, the following key system needs were
   616→identified: a cloud-hosted multi-facility platform accessible through a
   617→web browser without local server installation; real-time blood inventory
   618→management by blood group, Rhesus factor, and unit status; automated
   619→expiry monitoring with configurable alert thresholds; a broadcast
   620→request mechanism through which a facility in need can notify all
   621→registered facilities simultaneously and receive direct responses; blood
   622→unit reservation during inter-facility referral; role-based access
   623→control protecting different levels of system functionality; and
   624→reporting and dashboard tools for inventory visibility and operational
   625→oversight.

   926→3.3 System Design
   927→
   928→3.3.1 Architectural Design
   929→
   930→The Cloud-Based Blood Bank Management System is built on a three-tier
   931→client-server architecture hosted on a cloud platform. This architecture
   932→separates the system into three distinct layers, each with a defined
   933→responsibility, improving maintainability, scalability, and security.
   934→
   935→Presentation Layer (Client Tier)
   936→
   937→The presentation layer is the user-facing component of the system,
   938→delivered as a responsive web application accessible through any
   939→standard web browser on both desktop and mobile devices. The frontend is
   940→built using HTML5, CSS3, and JavaScript. Upon login, the system
   941→identifies the user's role and renders only the modules and navigation
   942→items applicable to that role, in line with the TAM-informed design
   943→principles described in Chapter Two. No software installation is
   944→required at the user's end, making the system immediately accessible to
   945→any facility with internet access.
   946→
   947→Application Layer (Business Logic Tier)
   948→
   949→The application layer is the server-side component that handles all
   950→business logic, data processing, and communication between the client
   951→and the database. It is implemented using Python with the Django web
   952→framework, exposing a RESTful API that the frontend communicates with
   953→over HTTPS. The application layer handles user authentication and
   954→session management, role-based permission enforcement, blood unit
   955→lifecycle management, expiry monitoring, internal blood request
   956→processing, and the broadcast request workflow.
   957→
   958→The broadcast request workflow is a core architectural feature of the
   959→application layer. When a blood bank officer submits a network blood
   960→request, the application server broadcasts a notification to all
   961→registered facilities. Facilities with matching available blood units
   962→receive an alert on their dashboard. Blood bank officers at responding
   963→facilities review the request and submit responses through the API. The
   964→requesting facility sees all responses in real time and selects the
   965→preferred option. Upon selection, the application server updates the
   966→status of the chosen unit to Reserved at the responding facility for the
   967→duration of the reservation period.
   968→
   969→Data Layer (Database Tier)
   970→
   971→The data layer consists of a relational database hosted on the cloud,
   972→implemented using MySQL. All system data, including facility records,
   973→user accounts, blood unit records, donor information, internal requests,
   974→network requests, responses, and audit logs, are stored in the database.
   975→The cloud-hosted database provides automated backups, point-in-time
   976→recovery, and replication for high availability, ensuring that blood
   977→inventory data is never permanently lost due to hardware failure.
   978→
   979→The three tiers communicate as follows: the user's browser sends HTTPS
   980→requests to the Django application server; the application server
   981→processes the request, applies business logic and permission checks,
   982→queries or updates the database as required, and returns a JSON
   983→response; the browser renders the response as an updated user interface.
   984→All communication between the browser and the application server is
   985→encrypted using HTTPS.
   986→
   987→3.3.2 Database Design
   988→
   989→The database schema is designed around the core entities of the blood
   990→bank system and their relationships. The nine primary entities and their
   991→key attributes are described below:
   992→
   993→Facilities
   994→
   995→Stores information about each registered hospital or clinic on the
   996→network. Key attributes include a unique facility identifier, facility
   997→name, facility type (Clinic, District Hospital, Regional Hospital,
   998→Teaching Hospital), physical location, contact information, registration
   999→date, and status (Pending, Active, Suspended).
  1000→
  1001→Users
  1002→
  1003→Stores staff account information for all registered users across all
  1004→facilities. Key attributes include a unique user identifier, a foreign
  1005→key referencing the facility, full name, email address, bcrypt-hashed
  1006→password, role (System Administrator, Blood Bank Officer, Laboratory
  1007→Technician, Hospital Administrator), and account status. The role field
  1008→governs access permissions throughout the system.
  1009→
  1010→Donors
  1011→
  1012→Maintains basic records of blood donors registered at each facility. Key
  1013→attributes include a unique donor identifier, a foreign key referencing
  1014→the registering facility, donor name, contact details, date of birth,
  1015→blood group, Rhesus factor, and date of last donation. This table
  1016→supports traceability and enables staff to contact a donor if a
  1017→post-donation issue is identified.
  1018→
  1019→Storage Units
  1020→
  1021→Records the physical storage equipment within each facility. Key
  1022→attributes include a unique storage unit identifier, a foreign key
  1023→referencing the facility, storage unit name or label, temperature
  1024→category (Refrigerated: 1 to 6 degrees Celsius; Frozen: minus 18 degrees
  1025→Celsius and below; Room Temperature: 20 to 24 degrees Celsius), and
  1026→maximum capacity. This supports appropriate storage assignment for
  1027→different blood component types.
  1028→
  1029→Blood Units
  1030→
  1031→The central inventory table of the system. Key attributes include a
  1032→unique unit identifier, foreign keys referencing the facility, the
  1033→donor, and the assigned storage unit, blood group, Rhesus factor,
  1034→component type (Whole Blood, Red Blood Cells, Platelets, Fresh Frozen
  1035→Plasma), collection date, expiry date, current status (Available,
  1036→Reserved, Issued, Expired, Discarded), and a foreign key referencing the
  1037→staff member who registered the unit. Status transitions are validated
  1038→by the application layer to prevent invalid state changes.
  1039→
  1040→Internal Blood Requests
  1041→
  1042→Records blood requests submitted from clinical wards or departments
  1043→within the same facility. Key attributes include a unique request
  1044→identifier, foreign keys referencing the facility and the requesting
  1045→user, blood group, Rhesus factor, quantity, urgency level (Routine,
  1046→Urgent, Emergency), patient reference, request timestamp, status
  1047→(Pending, Approved, Issued, Rejected, Cancelled), and a foreign key
  1048→referencing the blood unit eventually issued.
  1049→
  1050→Network Blood Requests
  1051→
  1052→Records broadcast blood requests submitted by a facility seeking blood
  1053→from the wider network. Key attributes include a unique request
  1054→identifier, foreign keys referencing the requesting facility and the
  1055→requesting user, blood group, Rhesus factor, quantity, urgency level,
  1056→broadcast timestamp, expiry timestamp, and status (Open, Partially
  1057→Responded, Reserved, Fulfilled, Expired, Cancelled).
  1058→
  1059→Network Request Responses
  1060→
  1061→Records each response submitted by a facility in reply to an open
  1062→network request. Key attributes include a unique response identifier,
  1063→foreign keys referencing the network request, the responding facility,
  1064→and the specific blood unit being offered, response timestamp, and
  1065→status (Pending, Selected, Rejected, Withdrawn).
  1066→
  1067→Audit Logs
  1068→
  1069→Records all significant data modification events for traceability and
  1070→regulatory compliance. Key attributes include a unique log identifier, a
  1071→foreign key referencing the user who performed the action, the action
  1072→type (Create, Update, Status Change, Delete), the affected record type
  1073→and identifier, a timestamp, and the user's IP address. Audit logs are
  1074→write-once and cannot be modified or deleted by any user role, ensuring
  1075→a tamper-resistant record of all system activity.
  1076→
  1077→The relationships between these entities are structured as follows: each
  1078→Facility has many Users, Donors, Storage Units, Blood Units, Internal
  1079→Requests, and may create many Network Requests and submit many Network
  1080→Responses. Each Blood Unit belongs to one Facility and one Storage Unit,
  1081→references one Donor, and may be linked to one Internal Request or one
  1082→Network Response. Each Network Request belongs to one Facility and may
  1083→have many Network Responses. These relationships are enforced through
  1084→foreign key constraints in the database, maintaining referential
  1085→integrity across all tables.
  1086→
  1087→3.3.3 User Interface Design
  1088→
  1089→The user interface of the Cloud-Based Blood Bank Management System is
  1090→designed around the principles of role-based visibility, minimal
  1091→navigation depth, and clarity under time pressure. Following the
  1092→Technology Acceptance Model framework applied in Chapter Two, every
  1093→interface decision was guided by the objectives of maximising perceived
  1094→usefulness and perceived ease of use for blood bank staff working in
  1095→busy clinical environments.
  1096→
  1097→Login Page
  1098→
  1099→The login page is the entry point to the system for all users. It
  1100→presents a clean, minimal form with fields for email address and
  1101→password, alongside the system name. Upon successful authentication, the
  1102→server identifies the user's role and redirects them to the appropriate
  1103→role-based dashboard. Failed login attempts are rate-limited to prevent
  1104→brute-force attacks.
  1105→
  1106→Role-Based Dashboard
  1107→
  1108→Each authenticated user lands on a dashboard personalised to their role.
  1109→The Blood Bank Officer dashboard displays the most operationally
  1110→critical information at a glance: a summary of current blood stock by
  1111→group and Rhesus factor with indicators for low stock levels, a list of
  1112→units expiring within the alert threshold, pending internal blood
  1113→requests awaiting processing, and open network requests requiring
  1114→attention. The Hospital Administrator dashboard prioritises
  1115→facility-level overview reports and staff management. Nurses and doctors
  1116→see a simplified view focused on submitting and tracking their blood
  1117→requests.
  1118→
  1119→Blood Inventory Page
  1120→
  1121→The inventory page presents a searchable and filterable list of all
  1122→blood units registered at the facility. Staff can filter by blood group,
  1123→Rhesus factor, component type, status, and expiry date range. Each unit
  1124→row displays its key details and current status. Blood bank officers can
  1125→add new units through a clearly labelled button that opens a structured
  1126→data entry form with server-side validation to prevent incomplete
  1127→records.
  1128→
  1129→Network Request Page
  1130→
  1131→The network request page is the interface through which a blood bank
  1132→officer initiates a broadcast request. The form captures blood group,
  1133→Rhesus factor, quantity, urgency level, and an optional note to
  1134→responding facilities. Once submitted, the page transitions to a live
  1135→responses view where incoming responses from other registered facilities
  1136→appear as they are submitted, showing the responding facility's name,
  1137→location, the offered unit's details, and a selection button. This
  1138→real-time response view is the primary interface through which the
  1139→broadcast-and-response workflow is experienced by the requesting
  1140→facility.
  1141→
  1142→Reports Page
  1143→
  1144→The reports page allows hospital administrators and blood bank officers
  1145→to generate and export inventory and usage reports. Users select a
  1146→reporting period and report type, and the system generates a summary
  1147→showing stock levels, units issued, units expired or discarded, and
  1148→network request activity. Reports can be printed or exported for
  1149→submission to management or regulatory bodies.
  1150→
  1151→3.3.4 Security Considerations
  1152→
  1153→Security is a foundational concern in the design of the Cloud-Based
  1154→Blood Bank Management System, given that the system processes sensitive
  1155→personal data belonging to blood donors. The following security measures
  1156→are incorporated into the system design:
  1157→
  1158→Authentication and Session Management
  1159→
  1160→All users authenticate using an email address and password. Passwords
  1161→are hashed using bcrypt before storage in the database, ensuring that
  1162→plaintext passwords are never stored. Upon successful login, the server
  1163→issues a session token that the browser stores securely. Sessions expire
  1164→after a defined period of inactivity, requiring the user to
  1165→re-authenticate.
  1166→
  1167→Role-Based Access Control
  1168→
  1169→Every API endpoint on the application server enforces role-based
  1170→permission checks before processing a request. A nurse or doctor can
  1171→submit a blood request but cannot access inventory management or donor
  1172→records. A laboratory technician can update blood unit screening results
  1173→but cannot approve or issue a unit. A blood bank officer can manage
  1174→inventory, process requests, and interact with the network request
  1175→workflow. A hospital administrator can manage staff accounts and view
  1176→reports but cannot modify blood unit records. These role boundaries are
  1177→enforced at the server level and cannot be bypassed through client-side
  1178→manipulation.
  1179→
  1180→Data Encryption in Transit
  1181→
  1182→All communication between the user's browser and the application server
  1183→is encrypted using HTTPS with TLS 1.2 or higher. This protects blood
  1184→unit data, donor personal information, and authentication credentials
  1185→from interception during transmission. The system will not serve content
  1186→over unencrypted HTTP connections.
  1187→
  1188→Input Validation and SQL Injection Prevention
  1189→
  1190→All user-submitted data is validated on both the client side and the
  1191→server side before processing. The Django ORM is used exclusively for
  1192→database queries, which parameterises all inputs and provides built-in
  1193→protection against SQL injection attacks. Input fields are constrained
  1194→to their expected data types and formats, with server-side rejection of
  1195→any input that does not conform to defined validation rules.
  1196→
  1197→Audit Trail
  1198→
  1199→All significant data modifications, including the creation and status
  1200→changes of blood units, donor records, blood requests, and network
  1201→requests, are recorded in the Audit Logs table. Each entry captures the
  1202→user, action, affected record, timestamp, and IP address. Audit records
  1203→are write-once and are not modifiable or deletable by any user role,
  1204→ensuring a tamper-resistant record of all system activity.
  1205→
  1206→Compliance with Ghana's Data Protection Act 2012
  1207→
  1208→The system is designed to comply with Ghana's Data Protection Act, 2012
  1209→(Act 843). Donor registration forms include explicit consent
  1210→acknowledgement for the recording and processing of personal health
  1211→data. Personal data is collected only for the purposes of blood bank
  1212→operations and is not shared beyond the registered healthcare facilities
  1213→on the network without additional explicit consent. These measures
  1214→collectively ensure that the system fulfils its legal obligations under
  1215→Ghanaian law.
  1216→
  1217→3.4 Conclusion
  1218→
  1219→This chapter presented the complete system specification and design of
  1220→the Cloud-Based Blood Bank Management System. The Agile methodology was
  1221→adopted for development, structured across four iterative sprints to
  1222→allow the system scope to evolve in response to ongoing research
  1223→findings and supervisor guidance. The requirements analysis, grounded in
  1224→the project's primary survey research and field interview, produced
  1225→twenty functional requirements and ten non-functional requirements
  1226→covering inventory management, expiry monitoring, internal and
  1227→cross-facility blood requests, role-based access, reporting, and
  1228→security.
  1229→
  1230→The stakeholder analysis identified six distinct user roles with clearly
  1231→defined system interactions. Ten use cases were documented to describe
  1232→the core workflows the system is built to support, with the broadcast
  1233→network request workflow representing the system's most significant
  1234→functional contribution to existing blood management practice in Ghana.
  1235→The system design presented a three-tier cloud architecture, a
  1236→relational database schema covering nine primary entities, a
  1237→TAM-informed user interface design built around role-based dashboards
  1238→and minimal navigation depth, and a comprehensive security framework
  1239→compliant with Ghana's Data Protection Act 2012.
  1240→
  1241→Together, these specifications and designs form the complete technical
  1242→blueprint for the system whose implementation and testing are described
  1243→in Chapter Four.
